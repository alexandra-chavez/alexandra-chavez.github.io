We are excited to share that our new paper is now available in <i>Proceedings of the Royal Society B</i>.
Using 56 genotypes of the clonal duckweed <i>Spirodela polyrhiza</i>, we assessed how copper excess in previous generations alters the fitness and phenotypes of offspring several generations later.
We found that transgenerational fitness effects differed consistently among genotypes and also depended on whether copper stress returned.
More importantly, we found that this transgenerational variation may be predicted from each genotype’s own resistance to copper excess and its reproductive rate.
After ancestral stress, more susceptible genotypes produced offspring that performed worse without recurring stress but resisted copper excess better when the stress returned, likely by protecting their photosystem II.
Our results show that transgenerational fitness effects are heritable, variable, and may be predictable from the genotype’s stress resistance.
